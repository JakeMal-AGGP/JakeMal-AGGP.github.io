AR Traffic Game



Team size: Solo

Platform: PC

Engine: Unity/Vuforia

My role: Programmer/Designer



About the project

This project was created as an exercise in utilizing Augmented Reality. It runs on Vuforia inside of Unity using a Target Image. It was a solo project developed by myself. The objective of the game is simple; you are overseeing traffic at an intersection, and must control the stoplights to allow traffic through without any accidents. If two cars collide, you lose. Also, if traffic gets too backed-up, you also lose. You have a score that is determined by how many cars make it safely through the intersection. Originally it was planned for there to be different types of cars that each had their own unique aspects to them. For example, a bus that took up more space and drove slower, or a motorcycle that took up less space and drove faster. This was in the game shortly during early versions, however was ultimately removed due to complications within the code, as well as time restraints. A planned feature that never made it off the drawing bored was for pedestrians to walk across the crosswalk, as another obstacle. This too, however, was cut due to time.





The technical aspects

This project was a mix of familiar and new for me. It used the Unity engine, which I was very familiar with, however the added aspects of Vuforia and AR added a nice "newness" I hadn't yet experienced or worked with. Vuforia itself was easy to set up and get working, however introduced its own quirks into the workspace that I hadn't yet dealt with. Namely was its perception of the gamespace. Very early into development I ran into a massive brickwall where I was having difficulty properly spawning in and positioning the cars. This hindered development for a good while while the solution seemed out of grasp. That was until I realized how simple yet illusive the issue was: gamespace. Since the game world is determined and projected through my phones camera, the "gamespace" was constantly changing based on where my phone was looking. I had been spawning and positioning the cars based on where my phone was looking, not relative to where they needed to be inside the actual gamespace. This meant that they were spawning in incredibly weird places, far off from where their original spawn points were supposed to be. The solution itself was painfully easy (compared to how long this brickwall of an issue had been plaguing me for): all I had to do was change a "position" value to "localPosition". That is, I should have been spawning the cars in locally to the gamespace, not my phones perception of the gamespace. After this massive hill was overcome, the rest of the development period went rather smoothly. A few hiccups here and there, but nothing that couldn't be solved in diligent time.
